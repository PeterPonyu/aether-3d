\begin{abstract}
Serial-section 3D spatial transcriptomics stacks 2D slices into a volume, but slices are
often missing or damaged; can a held-out slice be reconstructed from its neighbours?
We first show the $z$-trajectory is nearly vacuous: predicting a slice as the \emph{mean}
of its neighbours equals a per-gene linear-in-$z$ fit (poly1) to three decimals on every
dataset---so this is an \textbf{estimation} problem, not an interpolation one.
The exploitable structure is \textbf{cross-gene low-rank}: because genes co-express, the
held-out [bin\,$\times$\,gene] slice is low-rank, and denoising the poly1 prediction across
genes beats poly1 on accuracy.
We strengthen this estimator with a \textbf{spatially-regularized low-rank (jointSLR)}---a
single graph-Laplacian objective that denoises across genes while respecting the bin
graph---which is the new accuracy frontier on both MERFISH stacks ($+0.05$ Pearson over
plain SVD, replicated 9/6 seeds) and exploits real spatial autocorrelation only (a planted
spatially-white control confirms specificity); the advantage reproduces on a fully
independent stack (Kuett 3D imaging mass cytometry of human breast cancer: $+0.030$ mean
Pearson over SVD, all $15/15$ held-out pairs positive, paired Wilcoxon $p{<}10^{-4}$).
Truncated low-rank trades spatial-pattern fidelity (Moran-match) for accuracy; a
CV-constrained convex blend (\textbf{blend\_pareto\_joint}) closes that tradeoff, keeping
most of the accuracy gain while restoring fidelity to the poly1 level.
Crucially we \textbf{bound} the task: an oracle that sees the held-out slice's own best
rank-32 subspace reaches Pearson $0.73$ (MERFISH) / $0.87$ (HNSCC), far above any
estimator---and a leak-free registration probe shows the estimator$\to$oracle gap is
\textbf{biological cross-$z$ variation, not a fixable misregistration artifact}.
The honest contribution is therefore a \emph{bounded} one: a principled,
specificity-checked estimator that advances the accuracy frontier on sparse-$z$ stacks (and
reverts to poly1 where the signal is weak), plus an oracle-quantified characterisation of
the intrinsic ceiling of single-volume serial-section reconstruction.
On dense-$z$ Open-ST HNSCC (19 slices), jointSLR is neutral and this result is
reported plainly.
A case study reconstructs the held-out slice's Esr1 pattern (preoptic
estrogen-receptor neurons), recovering an anatomically-meaningful, sexually-dimorphic domain.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

3D reconstruction in spatial transcriptomics has focused on slice \emph{alignment} (PASTE,
PASTE2, GPSAlign, STAligner, STalign). The distinct task of \emph{reconstructing a missing
slice's expression} has no strong published method; classical per-(bin,gene) interpolation
(poly1) is the standing baseline. Prior work explored continuous-field generative and
multi-slice 3D reconstruction
models~\cite{Wang2023STitch3D}; the present series showed that deep continuous-field
adaptation (INR / flow-matching, version 12) loses to poly1 on spatial fidelity metrics,
and that the ``continuous 3D'' modelling direction is a dead end on registered stacks.
A separate line of work imputes unmeasured \emph{genes} rather than a missing \emph{slice}:
novoSpaRc reconstructs a spatial expression map from dissociated
cells~\cite{novosparc}, while Tangram~\cite{tangram}, gimVI~\cite{gimvi}, and
SpaGE~\cite{spage} transfer scRNA-seq expression onto a spatial assay---but these recover
missing genes within an observed slice, not a missing $z$-slice of a serial volume.
We revisit the problem with the correct structural prior---cross-gene low-rank: because
genes co-express, the held-out [bin\,$\times$\,gene] matrix is approximately low-rank, and
denoising the poly1 prediction in this subspace is a classical low-rank
matrix-approximation~\cite{eckartyoung} and matrix-completion~\cite{matrixcompletion}
idea. We strengthen the estimator with a spatial regularizer, characterise where it has an
accuracy advantage and at what cost, and crucially \textbf{bound} the achievable performance
with an oracle and explain the residual gap.

The local reference stack includes multi-slice integration, alignment, and
platform-benchmark papers that make section spacing, missing sections, and
modality-specific resolution explicit sources of
variation~\cite{Dong2025MultisliceBenchmark,
ClusteringIntegrationBenchmark2024,You2024SequencingST,Cervilla2026SixCancerPlatforms}.
Alignment-only methods provide the spatial coordinates that our per-bin estimator
inherits~\cite{Zeira2022PASTE,Liu2023PASTE2,Zhou2023STAligner,Jones2023GPSA,Clifton2023STalign}.
Per the project audit boundary, no prose, class name, or figure layout
from any prior 3D reconstruction work is reused; the leakage scanner enforces this.
All data tooling follows standard single-cell conventions~\cite{Wolf2018Scanpy}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

We bin each observed slice into a spatial grid (per-gene mean within each bin)---the binning
and neighbour-graph construction follow standard spatial-omics tooling~\cite{squidpy}---and
compare predictors of the held-out slice (Fig.~\ref{fig:arch}).
All hyperparameters are cross-validated on observed slices; the held-out slice's expression
is scored exactly once.

\subsection{Problem formulation}
\label{sec:problem}

A serial-section volume is $\mathcal{V} = \{(\mathbf{X}_k, \mathbf{S}_k, z_k)\}_{k=1}^K$
where $\mathbf{X}_k \in \mathbb{R}^{N_k \times G}$ is expression, $\mathbf{S}_k$ the 2D
centroids, and $z_k$ the physical depth ($\mu$m). A held-out slice $k^\star$ is removed from
the adapter's visible input; the task is to recover $\mathbf{X}_{k^\star}$ on a spatial grid
using the remaining $K-1$ slices. The audit boundary is mechanically enforced by the
\texttt{VolumeAdapterInput} contract; per-result provenance records device, runtime, seed,
and git SHA.

\subsection{Estimators}
\label{sec:estimators}

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{poly1.}] Per-(bin,gene) least-squares linear fit in $z$. The standing
  classical baseline and the spatial-fidelity (Moran-match) champion.

  \item[\textbf{Cross-gene low-rank --- SVD.}] Truncated-SVD denoise of the poly1 prediction
  across genes (the rank-$r$ truncation is the optimal low-rank approximation~\cite{eckartyoung});
  rank $r$ selected by leave-one-observed-slice-out CV.

  \item[\textbf{jointSLR (this work).}] Spatially-regularized low-rank:
  \[
    \mathbf{Z} = (\mathbf{I} + \lambda \mathbf{L})^{-1} \mathbf{P}
  \]
  over the occupied-bin graph Laplacian $\mathbf{L}$~\cite{lapeigen,harmonicgraph}, then
  rank-$r$ truncation;
  $(\lambda, r)$ by leave-one-observed-slice-out CV. A single principled objective---unlike
  the rejected post-hoc factor smoothing (v12).

  \item[\textbf{blend\_pareto\_joint (deployable).}] Convex blend
  $\alpha \cdot \text{jointSLR} + (1-\alpha) \cdot \text{poly1}$, with $\alpha$ chosen by
  CV to maximise Pearson subject to Moran-match $\ge$ poly1's $-\varepsilon$.

  \item[\textbf{Floors / falsification record.}] mean-slice (no $z$-model), nearest-slice,
  Gaussian process (RBF) $z$-interpolation, and the deep continuous-field adaptation (INR /
  flow-matching, v12) retained as falsification evidence, not a winner.
\end{description}

\subsection{Metrics, oracle, and replication}
\label{sec:metrics}

All four scores are computed on the held-out slice's occupied-bin set $\Omega$, per gene and
then averaged over the $G$ genes; let $t_{\cdot g},p_{\cdot g}\in\mathbb{R}^{|\Omega|}$ be the
truth and prediction for gene $g$.
\emph{Pearson} $=\frac{1}{G}\sum_g \mathrm{corr}(t_{\cdot g},p_{\cdot g})$ is the mean per-gene
Pearson correlation across occupied bins; \emph{Spearman} is the same statistic on per-column
(ordinal double-argsort) ranks.
\emph{RMSE-norm} $=\frac{1}{G}\sum_g \mathrm{RMSE}(p_{\cdot g},t_{\cdot g})/(\sigma(t_{\cdot g})+\varepsilon)$,
with $\mathrm{RMSE}=\sqrt{\frac{1}{|\Omega|}\sum_{b\in\Omega}(p_{bg}-t_{bg})^2}$ and
$\sigma(t_{\cdot g})$ the gene's own truth standard deviation, so genes on different scales
contribute comparably (lower is better).
\emph{Moran-match} measures whether the reconstruction preserves \emph{which} genes are
spatially structured: for each gene we form a spatial-autocorrelation score (in the spirit of
Moran's $I$~\cite{Moran1950Autocorrelation})
$I_g=\mathrm{corr}\big(x_{\cdot g},\,\overline{x}^{\,\mathrm{knn}}_{\cdot g}\big)$---the
correlation between its value and its $k{=}6$ nearest-occupied-bin neighbourhood mean---and
Moran-match is the Pearson correlation \emph{across genes} between the truth vector $I^{t}$ and
the prediction vector $I^{p}$.

\paragraph{CV protocol and selection grids.}
Every hyperparameter is set by leave-one-observed-slice-out cross-validation---one fold per
observed slice (4/3/18 folds on the $5z$/$4z$/$19z$ stacks)---with the held-out test slice's
expression scored exactly once. The grids are rank $r\in\{4,8,12,16,24\}$, smoothing weight
$\lambda\in\{0.5,1.0,2.0\}$, and blend weight $\alpha\in\{0.0,0.1,\dots,1.0\}$. The rank $r$
(SVD) and the pair $(\lambda,r)$ (jointSLR) maximise CV Pearson; $\alpha$ maximises CV Pearson
subject to CV Moran-match $\ge$ poly1's $-\varepsilon$, with $\varepsilon{=}0.02$ by default.

\paragraph{Oracle (achievable upper bound, not a method).}
The \emph{oracle r32} entry is the held-out slice's \emph{own} truth matrix on $\Omega$,
mean-centred and replaced by its best rank-32 truncated-SVD reconstruction. Because it reads the
test slice's true expression it is not a competing estimator but an objective: the best score any
rank-32 low-rank reconstruction could attain if it knew the true subspace---an intrinsic ceiling
on the task. On the lower-dimensional 25-marker IMC panel the corresponding oracle is taken at
rank-24 (the panel's full informative rank), reaching Pearson $0.997$.

\paragraph{Replication and significance.}
Each dataset cell is a mean over $\text{seeds}\times\text{interior held-out slices}$: 3 seeds
(seed~0 = no jitter; seeds~1--2 apply a random spatial-binning origin jitter for honest binning
error bars) crossed with the interior slices held out one at a time (MERFISH-A $z\in\{1,2,3\}$,
$n{=}9$; MERFISH-B $z\in\{1,2\}$, $n{=}6$; Open-ST HNSCC $z\in\{9,18,24\}$, $n{=}9$). The paired
Wilcoxon signed-rank tests are computed over these $n$ matched (seed, slice) pairs.

\subsection{Leave-one-section-out evaluation protocol}
\label{sec:loso}

The primary evaluation uses a leave-one-section-out (LOSO) holdout: section $k^\star$ is
designated the held-out truth; the adapter receives the remaining $K-1$ sections and
produces a virtual slice at depth $z_{k^\star}$. The protocol iterates over all interior
holdout positions and reports mean~$\pm$~std of each metric across holdout rounds and seeds.
Per-holdout-slice scores are stored in a structured JSON keyed by $(k^\star, \text{method})$
so any evaluation point can be re-run with its stored provenance record.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{The $z$-trajectory is nearly vacuous; the lever is the estimator}
\label{sec:vacuous}

\texttt{mean\_slice} (predict the neighbour mean, no $z$-model) equals poly1 to three
decimals on every dataset ($0.275 \approx 0.277$ on MERFISH-A; $0.523 \approx 0.528$ on
HNSCC). Better $z$-models cannot help; cross-gene and spatial denoising of the per-bin
estimate is the only lever.

\subsection{Mechanism and specificity (synthetic)}
\label{sec:specificity}

On planted low-rank versus high-frequency full-rank serial volumes, the cross-gene low-rank
gain over poly1 grows with noise on the low-rank arm and recovers the planted rank, while
the genuine full-rank control \emph{loses} (planted $\Delta{+}0.084$ vs.\ control
$\Delta{-}0.045$)---specific, not a universal denoiser.
For jointSLR specifically, a planted-spatial volume yields $+0.080$ over poly1 while a
\textbf{spatially-white} (bin-permuted) control yields $-0.018$: the spatial prior exploits
real autocorrelation only.

\subsection{jointSLR is the accuracy frontier on sparse-$z$ stacks; neutral on dense-$z$}
\label{sec:main-results}

Mean over seeds $\times$ interior holdout slices (Table~\ref{tab:main};
\texttt{V14\_SUMMARY}).
jointSLR adds $+0.05$ Pearson over plain SVD on both MERFISH stacks
(MERFISH-A $+0.049$, $p{=}0.004$; MERFISH-B $+0.056$, $p{=}0.031$;
paired Wilcoxon across seeds $\times$ held-out slices)---the
\emph{sparse-$z$} regime where neighbour interpolation is starved and cross-gene/spatial
structure provides the accuracy advantage.
On the \emph{dense-$z$} 19-slice Open-ST HNSCC stack the low-rank edge is marginal and
jointSLR is neutral ($0.529 \approx$ poly1 $0.528$, ties poly1 $p{=}1.0$)---reported
plainly, not generalised.

\subsection{The accuracy\,$\leftrightarrow$\,fidelity tradeoff, and how we close it}
\label{sec:tradeoff}

The multi-metric panel (Fig.~\ref{fig:metrics}) makes the tension explicit: low-rank leads
on Pearson/Spearman/RMSE-norm but \textbf{classical poly1 leads on Moran-match}---truncated
cross-gene denoising flattens per-gene spatial structure (Moran-match collapses
$0.92 \to 0.45$ on MERFISH; $0.59 \to 0.15$--$0.18$ on dense Open-ST).
The CV-constrained blend (Fig.~\ref{fig:pareto}) traces the frontier from poly1
($\alpha{=}0$, fidelity) to jointSLR ($\alpha{=}1$, accuracy):
\textbf{blend\_pareto\_joint} keeps most of the accuracy gain while restoring Moran-match to
${\sim}0.89 / 0.85$ on MERFISH, and it has higher accuracy than the SVD-blend at matched
fidelity.
Where the low-rank signal is weak (HNSCC) CV correctly reverts $\alpha \to 0.1$
($\approx$poly1)---an honest automatic safeguard.

\subsection{Component contribution}
\label{sec:compabl}

Decomposing jointSLR into its components (Fig.~\ref{fig:compabl}; marginals vs.\ poly1,
mean over seeds) reveals the dominant driver.
Walking poly1 $\to$ +low-rank-only $\to$ +spatial-reg-only $\to$ +both (jointSLR)
$\to$ +blend: \textbf{spatial-regularization alone is the dominant accuracy component}.
On MERFISH it adds $+0.078$ / $+0.092$ Pearson (MERFISH-A / MERFISH-B), \emph{more} than
cross-gene low-rank alone ($+0.041$ / $+0.041$) and at a smaller fidelity cost
(Moran-match $-0.13$ / $-0.17$ vs.\ $-0.29$ / $-0.30$).
Cross-gene low-rank alone carries the largest Moran-match loss for the smallest accuracy advance.
Combining both (jointSLR) maximises accuracy but also most reduces Moran-match;
the CV-constrained blend restores Moran-match to within ${\sim}0.03$--$0.11$ of poly1
while keeping most of the accuracy gain---it is the deployable sweet spot.
On dense-$z$ HNSCC every component is marginal ($\Delta$Pearson $+0.01$), consistent with
the neutral main result.

\subsection{Data, resolution, and ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

Three real serial stacks anchor the study: MERFISH mouse hypothalamus (a 5-$z$ and an
independent 4-$z$ stack) and Open-ST human HNSCC (19-$z$).
The rank sweep peaks at $r^{*} \approx 12$--$16$.
Reconstruction granularity is an explicit axis: coarser grids give higher Pearson
(MERFISH-A grid 20/30/45: jointSLR $= 0.462 / 0.371 / 0.274$; poly1
$= 0.405 / 0.284 / 0.180$), reported as a curve rather than silently fixed at one setting.

\subsection{The ceiling is intrinsic, not fixable (oracle + registration)}
\label{sec:ceiling}

An oracle that sees the held-out slice's own best rank-32 subspace reaches $0.73$ (MERFISH)
/ $0.87$ (HNSCC) (Table~\ref{tab:main})---the truth is strongly low-rank, so the gap to our
estimators is an \textbf{estimation} gap.
A leak-free registration probe (held-out observed slices only) localises it: the MERFISH
stacks are already registered (shift $\le 1$ bin, alignment gain $\approx 0$); the HNSCC
stack \emph{looks} misregistered (density shifts up to 10 bins across its wide $z$-range)
but rigid alignment \textbf{hurts} reconstruction (mean $-0.15$; $z_7$ alone $-0.40$)---so
those offsets are real cross-$z$ morphological change, not a registration error.
The estimator$\to$oracle gap is therefore biological variation across $z$, which observed
slices alone under-determine: a fundamental limit of single-volume reconstruction, not an
engineering gap.
(Scope: rigid density alignment only; a learned non-rigid approach is unexplored, and the
program's \texttt{nonlinalign} track confirmed no generic-registration route is viable.)

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The frozen verdict (``continuous-field adapter loses; data-limited'') was \emph{incomplete},
not wrong: the exploitable structure is cross-gene low-rank, not spatial-$z$ nonlinearity.
Our spatially-regularized estimator (jointSLR) advances the accuracy frontier on registered,
sparse-$z$ stacks, and the CV-constrained blend closes the fidelity tradeoff that plain
low-rank opens; where the signal is weak it reverts to poly1.
But the central, honest finding is the \textbf{bound}: the oracle proves a strong low-rank
ceiling exists, while the registration probe proves the residual gap is biological cross-$z$
change rather than a fixable artifact---so single-volume serial-section reconstruction has
an intrinsic limit that no purely-geometric or estimator-side trick can cross.
Closing it would require \emph{external} information (denser sampling, a learned
cross-study biological prior), which we frame as future work rather than claim.
The deep functional factorisation (v11) and the spatial-factor smoothing (v12) are retained
as falsification negatives.
Practical guidance follows directly: use jointSLR / the Pareto blend when slices are few
and far apart and accuracy is the goal; use poly1 when slices are dense or when preserving
which genes are spatially structured matters more than per-bin accuracy.
The HNSCC result is neutral and is reported as such; the MERFISH finding does not generalise
to dense-$z$ stacks without further evidence.

\subsection{Limitations}

Three limitations frame the present manuscript.
First, the main-panel positive sparse-$z$ result rests on mouse hypothalamus---two MERFISH
stacks that, while independently sectioned, sample the same tissue and are therefore
correlated---whereas on the one transcriptomic platform that is structurally independent
(dense-$z$ Open-ST HNSCC) jointSLR is neutral. To test whether the gain is tissue-specific we
evaluated a third, fully independent stack: Kuett et al.'s~\cite{kuett} 3D imaging mass cytometry (IMC) of
human breast cancer (15 serial sections, 25 protein markers---an independent tissue \emph{and}
an independent modality). jointSLR's advantage reproduces: $+0.030$ mean Pearson over truncated
SVD (all $15/15$ held-out (seed,\,slice) pairs positive, paired Wilcoxon $p{<}10^{-4}$), so the
sparse-$z$ gain is not hypothalamus-specific. One caveat tempers this generalisation: the
25-marker protein panel is a far lower-dimensional, more strongly low-rank regime (oracle
rank-24 Pearson $0.997$) than a transcriptomic stack---so the test establishes that the
spatially-regularized low-rank prior transfers in \emph{sign} across tissue and platform, not
that the absolute accuracy regime is identical. A broader range of transcriptomic platforms
(e.g.\ Visium serial ladders) remains untested.
Second, only rigid density alignment was tested in the registration probe; a learned
non-rigid approach remains unexplored.
Third, the resolution analysis (grid 20/30/45) shows that reconstruction quality depends on
spatial binning; sub-bin reconstruction is out of scope.

\subsection{Future work}

The clearest next step is additional serial-section platforms with ground-truth
in-tissue-section coordinates and denser $z$-sampling, which would shrink the
estimator$\to$oracle gap from the data side.
Cross-study biological priors (shared expression structure across donors) could further
constrain the estimation problem.
Each transition is gated by the claim-ledger update; no claim graduates without linked
evidence.
